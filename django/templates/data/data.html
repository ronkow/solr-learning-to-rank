In this section, we describe the grammar topics we cover and how we select the sentences.

1. Raw data

Our raw data are 1002 carefully curated questions and up to four answer choices per question. Excluding the wrong answer choices, we shall call a question and correct answer instance a sentence. From these raw sentences, we extract features and create datasets for the learning algorithm.

As of now, we have enough questions to cover only five types of words and phrases. Each question is classified (and labelled) into one of these five topics:

  • Prepositions
  • Conjunctions
  • Phrasal verbs
  • Verb tenses
  • Pronouns

Other than pronouns, the first four topics are somewhat related, in the sense that there are common individual words among the four sets of words. Some words (e.g. after, since) can be used as both prepositions and conjunctions. Phrasal verbs and verb tenses (e.g. "call for" and "is calling") contain common verbs. Phrasal verbs are formed by joining a verb and a preposition.

The scope of English grammar is extensive. We cover only a small subset of the words or phrases in each topic. For instance, pronouns could be classified into eight types and we cover only personal pronouns, and not all personal pronouns. There are more than a hundred commonly used prepositions and many more less common ones. We cover a small number of commonly used prepositions.

It must also be noted that grammar topic classification is no trivial task, because of the many sub-classes in each topic and also the ambiguities of the English language. Some nouns can be used as adjectives (e.g. school uniform). Some adjectives can be used as nouns (e.g. the blind uses Braille). Some words are used equally often as a noun or a verb (e.g. increase). The four basic word classes (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs) have many sub-classes (such as uncountable nouns and phrasal verbs). And other major classes such as pronouns and determiners (e.g. a, the) have their own sub-classes as well. This is an interesting attempt to visualise the taxonomy of English grammar.

2. Selection of sentences

To enable our models to learn syntax, we have selected sentences that are similar in syntax and semantics but different in words. For example, for the preposition "by":

  • I go to school by bus.
  • He travels to work by train.

Many sentences in our data are similar or repeated. The difference is just in the answer. For example:

  • I am doing my work now. (verb tenses)
  • I am doing my work now. (pronouns)

4. Topics

Prepositions

Prepositions show relationships between people, places or things. Prepositions often show relationships in space or time. There are hundreds of prepositions. We have selected a set of commonly used single-word and multi-word prepositions. For each preposition, there are at least five sentences.

Conjunctions

Conjunctions (e.g. and, or, but) connect words, phrases and clauses (groups of words containing a verb and a subject) in a sentence. The distinction between a conjunction and a preposition is subtle, and some words can be used as a conjunction and as a preposition. Consider the word after:

  • She became more independent after her mum passed away. (conjunction)
  • Her mum passed away after Christmas last year. (preposition)
For each selected conjunction, there are at least five sentences.

Verb tenses

Of the 12 major verb tenses, we cover all eight present and past tenses but only two of the future tenses. For each verb tense, there are at least twenty sentences.

Tense Example
Simple present She plays the piano every morning.
Present continuous She is playing the piano now.
Present perfect She has played this piece many times.
Present perfect continuous She has been playing the piano since morning.
Simple past He played basketball yesterday.
Past continuous He was playing basketball when it started to rain.
Past perfect He had played for his country only once before.
Past perfect continuous He had been playing basketball for an hour before it started to rain.
Simple future He will play in the first team.
Future continuous He will be playing in the next game.
Pronouns

A pronoun is a word that refers to a particular noun. Pronouns can be classified into personal (e.g. you, she), possessive (e.g. your, hers), reflexive (e.g. yourself, herself), indefinite (e.g. someone, anyone, nothing, everywhere), relative (e.g. ...which..., ...whom...) and interrogative (e.g. Which...?, Whom...?). We cover only a subset of all pronouns. For each pronoun, there are at least five sentences. The plural form of "yourself" ("yourselves") is not covered.
